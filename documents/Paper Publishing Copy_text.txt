Edge-AI and Multimodal Sensor Fusion–Based 
Smart Stick for Visually Impaired Individuals with 
Real-Time Obstacle Detection 
 
      Abstract— Independent mobility for visually impaired 
individuals is often limited by the inability of conventional 
assistive tools to provide early and reliable awareness of 
surrounding obstacles. While recent smart navigation aids 
incorporate sensing and artificial intelligence, many existing 
solutions depend on cloud computation or single -modality 
sensing, resulting in latency, privacy concerns, and reduced 
robustness in real -world environments. This work proposes a 
fully on-device smart stick based on Edge Artificial Intelligence 
and multimodal sensor fusion for real-time obstacle detection. A 
lightweight TinyML -based convolutional neural network 
processes visual input on a XIAO ESP32S3 microcontroller, 
while ultrasonic and infrared sensors supply complementary 
distance and proximity information. These modalities are 
combined using a decision -level fusion strategy to enhance 
detection reliability under varying environmental conditions. 
Experimental validation conducted in indoor and semi-outdoor 
settings demonstrates  an average detection accuracy of 0.89, 
with precision and recall values of 0.88 and 0.86, respectively. 
The system achieves inference latency below 125 ms and 
operates with low power consumption suitable for prolonged 
use. The results confirm the feasibility of deploying multimodal 
Edge-AI perception on resource-constrained assistive devices. 
       Keywords:    Edge Artificial Intelligence, Assistive 
Navigation, Multimodal Sensor Fusion, TinyML, Embedded 
Vision Systems, Real-Time Obstacle Detection 
I. INTRODUCTION  
 
Visual impairment has a significant influence on the self -
sufficiency and self -safety, particularly in the context of a 
new or a dynamically changing environment. According to 
global health reports, millions of people rely on assistive 
devices to move around in the space of daily activities, and in 
these cases, unidentified barriers, including furniture, stairs, 
or moving objects, may cause an accident and undermine 
confidence. Traditional mobility aids, such as white canes, 
provide tactile feedback over a short d istance only and in 
doing so require physical contact with the obstructions. 
Despite being essential tools, their sensing abilities are 
limited, which explains why it is important to have smart 
assistive technologies that can sense the surroundings that 
cannot be accessed instantly and provide early warnings.   
 
The last ten years have seen the emergence of tremendous 
advancements in artificial intelligence and embedded 
systems, which has triggered the growth of smart mobility 
support, incorporating electronic sensors and computational 
intelligence. The systems th at involve vision, especially, 
would offer high contextual details in terms of identification 
of object types and space definition in the surrounding. 
Similarly, the distance sensors, including ultrasonic and 
infrared sensors are commonly used due to their affordability 
and durability. However, many of the current assistive 
solutions are based on a single sense, making them vulnerable 
to noise in the environment like a low -light environment, 
reflective surfaces, or sensor noise. This means that in real 
life conditions, obstacle detection reliability is not coherent.   
 
A number of studies have explored the use of camera -based 
obstacle detection using deep learning models and have 
obtained promising results in controlled laboratory settings. 
In spite of these achievements, the methods often rely on 
cloud computing or supercomputers, therefore, adding to the 
latency, higher energy usage, and privacy issues. Other 
systems use ultrasonic or infrared sensors only, and they 
provide quick responses but do not provide semantic 
understanding of the environment. Such paradigms norm ally 
yield binary warnings without differentiating between 
different obstacles disparate types and contextual risks, hence 
restricting their use in challenging maneuvering exercises.

accuracy perce ption systems and the real -life assistive

II. LITERATURE REVIEW 
The research [1] provides a report on the research of AI-based 
smart cane technologies that are developed following the 
trend of adding the concept of artificial intelligence to 
assistive navigation technology to improve the perception of 
obstacles and user control. Though the work does a good job 
in classifying the sensing and intelligence methods used in 
smart canes, it rather focuses on the conceptual trends of 
design as opposed to practical embedded implementation. Its 
use in low-power, on-device applications is restricted by the 
use of high-level AI systems and the lack of discussion about 
real-time qualifications. Likewise, the study in [2] is an 
overview of navigation systems used by people with visual 
impairments and categorizes solutions based on th e sense 
modal and guidance techniques. Although the review 
contains useful taxonomies, it focuses more on system -level 
descriptions and does not include a critical assessment of 
computational feasibility, especially in the context of edge -
based implementation on microcontrollers. 
The article in [3] discusses the assistive systems in the 
challenge approach, and all constraints are found in the area 
of sensor reliability, environmental variability and user 
adaptability. Even though the authors acknowledge the nature 
of multimodal sensing, the review fails to explore the issue of 
sensor fusion achieved effectively within embedded 
limitations. A more technological view is given in [4], here 
they use emerging technologies like visible light 
communication and AI-based perception. Although the study 
has an innovative scope, it more or less discusses the 
infrastructure assisted solutions as well as the advanced 
mechanisms of communication which cannot be applied to 
portable and standalone smart sticks that are supposed to be 
used daily. 
In [5], bibliometric and narrative knowledge about wearable 
mobility aids is presented, and it can be seen that there is an 
increased interest in the research on smart wearables and 
obstacle detecting devices. Nevertheless, the research is 
focused on the t rends in publications and the further 
development of the research instead of the assessment of the 
algorithmic effectiveness or the performance in practice. 
Contrary, the systematic review in [6] evaluates the 
technologies of navigation in a more methodolo gically 
detailed manner; various sensing and feedback strategies are 
compared. However, the majority of systems examined 
depend on either vision intensive processing or external 
computing capabilities thus restricting their capability to 
operate continuously and with low power. 
This more narrow discussion on multimodal solutions is 
given in [7] where data fusion solutions to multi -sensor 
architectures serving blind and visually impaired users are 
compared in research prototypes and commercial products. 
Even though this work vivid ly illustrates the advantages of 
sensor combination, most of the systems that have been 
discussed utilize advanced fusion architectures or rely on 
high-level processing units, which makes them challenging 
to run on resource -constrained platforms. The focus  on 
human aspects is adopted in [8], where the author talks about 
wearable form factors and user comfort. Although it is 
important that the ergonomics and usability are taken into 
account, the study does not provide much information on the 
algorithmic desi gn or embedded intelligence which is why 
the gap between the user -centered design and the technical 
implementation exists. 
Systems that detect obstacles that are built into wearables are 
thoroughly discussed in [9], whereby several vision -based 
and sensor-based methods are compared. The authors point 
out that most systems have a limited range of detection or do 
not have semantic insight. Notably, the review observes that 
the few solutions that manage to achieve a tradeoff in 
accuracy, responsiveness and power efficiency, are not 
successful. This is consistent with the reason why we need to 
adopt lightweight Edge-AI models, and they can do real-time

III. PROPOSED SYSTEM 
 
The current methodology outlines an Edge AI -powered 
smart cane which integrates multimodal sensor fusion with 
intelligence gained through vision to provide people with 
visual impairment with real -time obstacle detection. As 
depicted in Figure 1, the general sy stem architecture is 
envisioned to be a brief, multilayered pipeline that enables 
sensing, local intelligence processing, decision making, and 
user feedback and is capable of running with the 
computational and energy constraints that are characteristic of 
embedded platforms. This methodology puts a premium on 
on-device computing to reduce latency, eliminate network 
connectivity to achieve user privacy.

The weighting coefficients α, β, and γ were selected 
empirically through preliminary experiments to balance the 
contributions of visual confidence, distance measurement, 
and infrared proximity sensing. The coefficients were 
normalized such that α + β + γ = 1, ensuring a consistent and 
interpretable fusion scale. Higher weight was assigned to the 
vision-based confidence score to preserve semantic 
awareness, while ultrasonic and infrared measurements were 
given complementary weights to enhance robustness unde r 
low-light conditions and near -field obstacle scenarios. 
Sensitivity analysis showed that small variations in these 
coefficients did not significantly affect overall detection 
performance, indicating stable fusion behavior. 
 
The proposed formulation would make sure that those 
challenges that are found to be highly perceived in terms of 
visual certainty and physical proximity are assigned a high 
risk, and those that are isolated or noisy are dampened. The 
decision logic subdivi des the obstacles based on the 
calculated risk score into various warning levels, that is, low, 
medium, and high risk. The use of threshold -based 
classification is to maintain transparency and predictability of 
system behaviour thus earning the trust of th e users. 
Decision-level fusion should be adopted due to the limited 
computational capacity of the embedded platform and the 
need to have a deterministic and real -time response which is 
not provided by complex probabilistic fusion techniques. 
After the decision is adjudicated, the system activates the user 
-feedback subsystem, which consists of a vibration motor or 
a buzzer. The strength or time dynamics of the feedback are 
adjusted according to the risk level assessed which results in 
non-visual communication that is easy to grasp. As an 
example, the greater the vibration frequency, the closer or 
more dangerous the obstacle can be, and the lesser, the 
further. This type of feedback paradigm reduces the cognitive 
load and allows individuals to r espond naturally in the 
process of navigation.

IV. IMPLEMENTATION AND RESULTS 
 
The effectiveness of the suggested Edge -AI and multimodal 
sensor fusion-based smart stick was tested in a sequence of 
controlled experiments that were aimed to replicate the 
setting of real Assistance navigation conditions. The test 
work was carried out in the  form of a prototype smart stick, 
which was applied to the XIAO ESP32S3 microcontroller, a 
camera module, an ultrasonic sensor, and an infrared sensor. 
The system of sensing, inference, and decision -making 
operations were performed on the local platfor m embedded 
in the system without reference to any connection to a cloud. 
The offline training of the vision model was done using 
COCO128 dataset and then quantization was used to optimize 
the model before deployment. The testing was performed 
both in an indoor and semi- out of doors setting with common 
components which include walls, furniture and human 
movement and several runs were conducted to include 
accountability of variation in the environment and sensor 
noise.  
 
The evaluation methodology was designed to assess not only 
detection accuracy but also the feasibility of deploying the 
proposed system as a real-world assistive device. The system 
was evaluated under indoor and semi -outdoor environments 
containing static and dynamic obstacles, including walls, 
furniture, and human movement. Multiple experimental runs 
were conducted to account for environmental variability and 
sensor noise, and all reported metrics represent averaged 
values. Performance was assessed using s tandard 
classification metrics —accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 -
score—to evaluate detection reliability, while inference 
latency and power consumption were measured to validate 
real-time responsiveness and energy efficiency. This multi -
dimensional evaluation framework ensures that the proposed 
system is validated not only in terms of perception 
performance but also with respect to practical deployment 
constraints such as low latency and battery -powered 
operation, which are critical for assistive navigation systems.

standard classification metrics, namely accuracy, precision,

coherent user experience.The proposed system has a smaller

compared to the c loud-based or high -power embedded

the complementary sensing modalities thereby reducing the 
limitations that come with the solitary vision  and distance 
sensors.The suggested system presents unquestionable 
benefits when compared to baseline assistive - navigation 
paradigms. Traditional smart sticks, which are limited to 
ultrasonic or infrared transducers, only, impose a limited 
level of enviro nmental perception and are prone to false 
positives. Vision only embedded solutions despite their more 
informational payload often face difficulties in real time 
execution and resilience in changing environmental 
conditions. The Edge -AI and multimodal sens or-fusion 
system seals this gap by combining semantic perception and 
trusted distance sensing, thus achieving a reasonable balance 
between accuracy, response latency and energy efficiency. 
From a feasibility perspective, the proposed system aligns 
with and extends earlier feasibility studies on embedded 
assistive navigation devices. Previous works have 
highlighted that achieving a balance between perception 
accuracy, response latency, and po wer efficiency remains a 
key challenge for wearable and portable assistive aids [9], 
[10]. The experimental results presented in this study 
demonstrate that such a balance is achievable using Edge -AI 
and decision -level sensor fusion on a microcontroller -class 
device. Unlike several prior approaches that rely on high -
complexity processing or external computation, the proposed 
system validates the feasibility of fully on-device intelligence 
while maintaining practical performance constraints. These 
findings r einforce the importance of feasibility -driven 
evaluations when translating assistive navigation research 
into deployable real-world solutions.

modality approaches aligns with findings from multimodal 
fusion studies [7], while demonstrating that effective fusion 
can be achieved without computationally complex 
architectures. These results highligh t the practical feasibility 
of combining TinyML-based vision and multimodal sensing 
for assistive navigation in real-world environments. 
V. CONCLUSION 
 
This paper presented an Edge -AI and multimodal sensor 
fusion–based smart stick designed to assist visually impaired 
individuals through reliable real -time obstacle detection. By 
integrating a TinyML-optimized vision model with ultrasonic 
and infrared sensi ng on a resource -constrained embedded 
platform, the proposed system achieves accurate 
environmental perception without reliance on cloud 
computing. Experimental results demonstrate that the fusion-
based approach improves detection accuracy while 
maintaining low inference latency and energy efficiency 
suitable for continuous daily use. The findings confirm that 
combining embedded vision with complementary distance 
sensing provides a practical balance between accuracy, 
responsiveness, and power consumption. F uture work will 
focus on extending the system to more complex outdoor 
environments, incorporating adaptive feedback mechanisms, 
and exploring continual learning strategies to further enhance 
long-term usability.
